Computers can't read words — but they can measure distance and angle. This is how a word becomes a point in space, and how two ideas are judged "similar."

01 — Embeddings

A word becomes a coordinate.

An embedding turns each word into a list of numbers — a vector. Words used in similar contexts get placed near each other. Watch raw text drop into a coordinate space where meaning becomes location.

Press play — each word flies to its place. Notice how king · queen and cat · dog cluster together, while rocket drifts off alone.
Idea

Similar meaning → nearby points

"Frock" lands next to "dress" because they appear in the same kinds of sentences, even though they share no letters.

Reality

Not 2 dimensions — 384

We draw 2 axes so we can see it. Real models (like all-MiniLM-L6-v2) use hundreds of dimensions per word.

02 — Cosine Similarity

Measure the angle, not the distance.

Pin one word — "dress" — as a fixed reference. Now compare it against different words. The closer another word's meaning, the smaller the angle. cosine similarity = cos(θ) runs from 1 (same direction) through 0 (unrelated) to −1 (opposite meaning).

Angle θ
Sweep the teal vector to compare "dress" against other words. A near word like "gown" sits at a tiny angle (cos ≈ 1); "rocket" sits near 90° (cos ≈ 0).
Why angle?

Length shouldn't matter

A long document and a short query can mean the same thing. Cosine ignores magnitude and asks only: do they point the same direction?

In practice

This powers semantic search

Embed the query, embed every product, return the items whose vectors sit at the smallest angle. Meaning-based matching, no keywords required.
